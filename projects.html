Formfor.me

TravelBot

Available at formfor.me, Formfor.me is PennApps XII's Best Social/Local Impact & Government Hack. An online solution for those who struggle with English and government forms. We take common IRS forms in PDF format, parse the form into plain text and apply foreign-language translation. We then display the form as easy-to-read questions to the user that they answer in simple one-line responses Finally, we auto-populate the PDF form and provide the completed form to the user. It's that simple!

We deployed the Formfor.me backend using Microsoft Azure as a Python/Flask API on an Ubuntu VM. Our front-end was deployed on Heroku using Angular.JS, Express.JS, and Node.JS along with JavaScript/HTML/CSS. We also used many PDF libraries available in Python such as PDFMiner, PDFTK, and FDFGen. Machine translation (to foreign languages) was handled using the Yandex API.

TravelBot

TravelBot

Available at travelbot2.herokuapp.com, TravelBot is a web tool that creates a customized itinerary for the city of Chicago based on user feedback. TravelBot presents the user with a possible tourist destination and the user decides whether it is a place they would like to visit. Based on these choices, TravelBot becomes more attuned to the user's preferences and suggests places that it thinks the user would enjoy.

TravelBot utilizes Node.js, Angular.js, MongoDB, and Bootstrap. I worked in collaboration with Craig Olson, Sachin Lal, Ornella Hernandez, and Brittany Magee.

Top 100 Predictor

Top 100 Predictor

The Top 100 Predictor is a project that analyzes which Machine Learning classifiers best predict the projected popularity of a song. For our project, we took Spotify's Top 100 songs from 2014 and pulled 1,000+ other songs from 2014 so we could see the accuracy of our results. We utilized the Spotify and Echonest song APIs for our song data and Weka, a collection of ML classifiers. We found that Naive Bayes outperformed K* and Decision Trees with the greatest amount of true hits (songs correctly classified as hits), although Decision Trees classified the greatest amount of true misses (songs correctly classified as non-hits).

I collaborated on this project with Jaiveer Kothari, Kevin Jin, and Ally Cody. See our report at http://k-jin.github.io/Top100Predictor/.

BuzzKill

BuzzKill

Built at HackIllinois 2015, BuzzKill is a Pebble smartwatch application that detects what website you are visiting on your laptop, sees whether it is a time-wasting website (e.g. Facebook, Twitter, Imgur, etc.), and if it is, starts a timer on the Pebble that tracks how long you stay on the webpage. Additionally, the Pebble buzzes every 15 seconds to remind you that you are still wasting time on that page. When you exit the time-wasting website and go to a kosher website (e.g. Google, Wikipedia, or any website that promotes productivity), the timer and incessant buzzing stop.

I worked in collaboration with Jaiveer Kothari, Kevin Jin, and Yoni Pinto. Check out our DevPost about the project at http://devpost.com/software/buzzkill-yaj2q.

HeartFlash

HeartFlash

Built at TreeHacks 2015, HeartFlash is an iOS app that uses the iPhone flashlight to monitor one's heartbeat (bpm) and suggest exercises based on the bpm and user-entered age. The user places their fingure over the flash for 15-20 seconds. Once the bpm is calculated, the user is presented with exercises that will raise their bpm to a certain number as well as with explanations on how to perform said exercises.

HeartFlash is special to me as it is my first iOS application. I worked in collaboration with Jess Lee. Check out our DevPost about the project at http://devpost.com/software/heartflash.

Shake N' Quake

Shake N' Quake

Shake N' Quake is an HTML5 game that won 1st place (woo!) at the International Women's Hackathon at Northwestern University in 2014, hosted by Microsoft. The game's purpose is to educate disaster-relief organizations and communities on what is the appropriate protocol during an earthquake. Our game has three levels - Phase 1: Collect items for your inventory before the earthquake, Phase 2: Avoid falling objects and find shelter during the earthquake, and Phase 3: Avoid hazards and find the nearest Red Cross Disaster-Relief tent after the earthquake.

I worked in collaboration with Bryanna Yeh, Yvonne Cao, and Robin Brewer.
